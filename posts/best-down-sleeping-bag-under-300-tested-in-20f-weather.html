Buying Guide

When selecting a down sleeping bag under $300, focus on fill power (600-650 is the sweet spot for this price range), temperature rating accuracy, and construction quality. Look for continuous baffles or wave construction rather than sewn-through seams, which create cold spots. Consider your typical sleeping position – mummy bags maximize warmth-to-weight ratio but restrict movement, while roomier cuts sacrifice some thermal efficiency for comfort.

Don't overlook practical features like draft collars, full-zip draft tubes, and quality zippers that won't fail in cold conditions. Down treatment (like DriDown or DownTek) adds value for humid environments but isn't essential for dry climates. Weight matters for backpacking, but car campers can prioritize durability and roominess over absolute weight savings. Always choose a bag rated 10-15 degrees below your expected low temperature for comfortable sleep.

Frequently Asked Questions

How warm is a 20°F sleeping bag in real-world conditions?

A 20°F rated bag will typically keep most sleepers comfortable down to about 30-35°F. The rating represents survival temperature, not comfort temperature. For comfortable sleep, add 10-15 degrees to the rating. Cold sleepers should size down in rating, while warm sleepers can push closer to the limit.

Is down or synthetic insulation better for a $300 sleeping bag?

Down offers superior warmth-to-weight ratio and compressibility, making it ideal for backpacking. However, synthetic insulation performs better when wet and is easier to maintain. For most three-season use in dry conditions, down provides better value. Choose synthetic for wet climates or if you prefer easy machine washing.

What's the difference between 600-fill and 650-fill down?

Fill power measures down's loft – higher numbers indicate more insulating air space per ounce. 650-fill down is about 8% more efficient than 600-fill, meaning you need less weight to achieve the same warmth. The difference is noticeable in ultralight applications but less critical for car camping.

How should I care for a down sleeping bag?

Store uncompressed in a large cotton sack or hung up. Wash only when necessary using down-specific detergent in a front-loading machine. Dry thoroughly with tennis balls or dryer balls to restore loft. Avoid compression for extended periods and keep away from moisture during storage.
